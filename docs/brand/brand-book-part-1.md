# Brand Book Part 1: The Foundation

Working draft — May 1, 2026

---

This document captures the foundation of the Formulation Wizard brand: who the founders are, what the company is trying to become, who it serves, what those people struggle with, and what the company believes about its industry.

It is the first of several parts. Voice and tone, visual identity, positioning, and the practical brand-system pieces (templates, asset specifications, formats) come next.

Everything in this document was developed through founder interviews and is recorded in the founders' own language wherever possible. Phrases marked verbatim are to be preserved exactly as written — they are the raw material for marketing copy, the about page, and any place the brand needs to sound like itself.

---

## 1. The Founders

Formulation Wizard was built by two food people.

One came up through the kitchen. He trained at the French Culinary Institute and spent his career as a chef and R&D developer, then as a new product development director at co-packers and product manufacturers. His technical work in formulation and regulatory affairs is grounded in something most product development tools don't accommodate: deep sensory and flavor mastery. He understands formulation through the palate, the nose, and the texture of a finished product, not just through specs and data.

The other came at the food world from a different direction — fifteen years as a visionary on food projects, connecting agricultural and artisan production to culinary art. Roles spanning marketing director, R&D chef, brand ambassador, catering designer, front-of-house lead. Storytelling, trend awareness, market instinct. Currently a sustainability expert consultant for global foodservice management companies.

Together, they tried to launch their own CPG product lines and ran straight into the wall everyone in this industry hits: the technical information backlog, the spec management, the fragmented stack of tools that can't keep up with how product development actually moves. They had also been working with small food businesses facing exactly the same wall.

The founder who lived inside the regulatory and formulation stack at scale started thinking it would be great if the process were simpler and easier to organize. **Get the calculations, specs, and regulations simplified, so we can get back to making products that taste amazing.** (verbatim)

That sentence is the heart of why Formulation Wizard exists. The tool isn't an end in itself. It clears the operational underbrush so the actual creative work — making food that tastes amazing — gets the attention it deserves.

## 2. The Vision

**We become the technical and regulatory backbone the entire industry runs on — from the smallest startup to the largest manufacturer — because we are the only ones who built the tool for the whole stack instead of carving out a niche.**

This is the ten-year horizon. Not a revenue number, not a user count. The thing the founders would actually be proud of: that the food industry's product development work — at every scale — runs on infrastructure that finally treats the work the way operators experience it.

A few notes on what this vision implies:

**Backbone, not brand.** Backbone things are quiet, reliable, deeply credible, and earn trust through competence rather than marketing flash. The brand has to feel that way. Not playful. Not scrappy small-brand. Substantial, calm, technically credible, aesthetically serious.

**Whole stack, not niche.** Most existing tools — Recipal, Genesis R&D, the in-house systems at large manufacturers, the spreadsheets at small ones — have carved out a slice of the work. The bet is that a tool built for the whole stack wins because the industry has been suffering from fragmentation. This is a competitive thesis, not just an aspiration.

**Every scale, in spirit, from day one.** The first paying customers may come from a narrower wedge. The brand voice, the visual feel, and the positioning have to land with both a chef-founder running a five-person hot sauce brand and a director at a 500-person manufacturer. Operator language is universal at every scale; what changes is the tooling and the politics, not the vocabulary.

## 3. The Customer

The brand voice doesn't speak to a range. It speaks to a person. Two real personas anchor the work — one in the middle of the spectrum, one at the larger end. A third (small-end, chef-founder) may be added later.

### Persona 1: Ram

Ram is a new product development director at a small-to-midsized co-packer that specializes in sauces. He is competent, director-level, juggling many concurrent projects, and not someone who needs hand-holding.

His clients are chef-driven brands in development. He works the full arc with them: bench-top samples first, scaling culinary recipes for that application, then production runs when the client is ready. He scales recipes, sources ingredients, flags control points and lab work, runs production, and delivers nutritionals, ingredient statements, and packaged information out to label designers and to the client.

Ram's pain is fragmentation. He runs the work across a stack of tools and spreadsheets — costs in one place, formula specs in another, supplier info in a third. He has no real-time price adjustment model that scales alongside his nutritional panels and ingredient deliverables. He cannot click and see how adjusting a volume changes his hazard analysis. **He goes to bed with them all dispersed across multiple screens.** (verbatim)

In Ram's own voice: *"I spend so much time at my desk switching between spreadsheets and tools that I don't have time to meet with people face to face. When I go home at the end of the day I often resume working remotely so I can get organized before client meetings."* (verbatim)

What the tool gives Ram: consolidation and cognitive offload. Not "saves time." Something deeper. The work stops following him home in his head because the costs, specs, hazard analysis, supplier data, and labeling outputs all live in one place and update together when one variable changes. He gets back the part of his job that requires being in front of people — the relationships with clients, the face-to-face meetings, the work he got into this for in the first place.

### Persona 2: Paul

Paul works for a major food manufacturing company with a full suite of product lines. He is sophisticated about market trends, fluent in the strategic-product-development register, and operating inside an institution.

Paul has been tasked with developing a sauce that taps the clean-eating, health-conscious consumer. He has a price point he needs to target and sustainability metrics he needs to meet. He uses Formulation Wizard to work through several variables in parallel — ingredient pricing, conventional versus certified organic, non-GMO verified versus fair trade options, packaging options — developing multiple ideas quickly that he can bring to R&D ready to bench-test, and then deliver samples to the executive team in days rather than weeks.

Paul's pain is not fragmentation in the same way Ram's is. Paul has institutional infrastructure around him. His pain is the speed and parallelism the institution demands of him, given that the existing tools force him to work sequentially.

In Paul's own voice: *"The executives in our new product meeting often seem lost when I present spreadsheets, cost analysis, and sustainability outlines separately. It would be great if I could present the packet all in one."* (verbatim)

Paul's pain is that he is forced to deliver fragmented work to people who need integrated work. The cost analysis lives in one deck, sustainability in another, the spec in a third, and the executive in the room has to do the integration in their head in real time — and they often can't, or shouldn't have to.

What the tool gives Paul: parallel exploration and credibility upward. He can develop multiple defensible concepts in parallel, with real numbers behind each. He can walk into the executive review with integrated packets that decision-makers can actually act on. The tool makes him look prepared because he is prepared.

### What the two personas tell us together

Ram and Paul share enough that the brand voice can speak to both without splitting: both are competent, both are operators, both work in food, both value the integration of culinary and operational thinking.

They differ enough that the brand book has to be genuinely flexible: small co-packer dynamics versus large manufacturer dynamics, individual judgment versus committee defense, consolidation as the value versus optionality as the value.

Anything written for the brand that lands with both Ram and Paul is on the right track.

The deeper value claim that runs underneath both personas: the tool removes the friction that keeps operators away from the part of the work they got into this for. For Ram, that's the relationships and the craft. For Paul, that's developing products that actually deliver. For the founder, it's making products that taste amazing. Same emotional core, three faces.

## 4. What We Believe

Every strong brand has a point of view. These are the four beliefs that ground Formulation Wizard's stance in its industry.

**1. Fragmentation isn't a small-business problem. It runs the whole industry.** Even the largest manufacturers work with tools that cover only part of what they need. Most software companies in this space assume the big players have it figured out and the opportunity is at the small end. The gap is universal. The opportunity is to build for the whole stack at once.

**2. Compliance isn't a department that audits the work after it's done. It's part of the work itself, built in as the formulation takes shape.** The industry has been treating regulatory as a function that reviews finished output. We treat it as something that should be enforced as the formulation is being built, by the people doing the formulating.

**3. The tools should fit the operator. Not the other way around.** Operators going to bed with the work in their heads, presenting fragmented packets that executives can't follow — those are signs of a tool ecosystem that makes operators contort themselves to fit the tools' shape. The tool should fit the operator's shape. Sounds obvious. Isn't, in this category.

**4. The industry treats culinary creativity and operational rigor like two different worlds. We've spent our careers proving they aren't.** The chef-driven side and the regulatory-and-formulation side keep getting separated by translators and handoffs. Good product development is one job, not two. The tool should reflect that. So should the company.

## 5. Phrases to Preserve

Phrases recorded verbatim from the founders during this brand work. They are the raw material for marketing copy, the about page, the homepage hero, long-form content, and any place the brand needs to sound like itself rather than like a generic SaaS company.

> We are food people.

> Get back to making products that taste amazing.

> He goes to bed with them all dispersed across multiple screens.

> I spend so much time at my desk switching between spreadsheets and tools that I don't have time to meet with people face to face.

> The executives in our new product meeting often seem lost when I present spreadsheets, cost analysis, and sustainability outlines separately. It would be great if I could present the packet all in one.

These are not slogans. They are the texture of the brand's voice as the founders and the customers actually speak. Marketing copy should sound like it could have come from these phrases, or could lead into them naturally.

## What Comes Next

This document covers the foundation. The remaining brand work is organized into four more parts:

**Part 2 — Voice and tone.** Who the brand sounds like, who it doesn't, what words and phrases are off-limits, what tone the brand carries in three to five words, and what the relationship is between the brand and the reader. This is where the culinary background becomes the moat — the voice section, written first, gives every downstream piece of writing something to be in service of.

**Part 3 — Visual identity.** Codifying the logo system, color palette, typography, photography direction, and the role each element plays. The marketing site iterations from late April have established the visual direction; Part 3 turns it into a transferable system.

**Part 4 — Positioning.** The one-sentence version of what Formulation Wizard is. Competitive landscape — Recipal, Genesis R&D, in-house systems, spreadsheets — and how the brand frames itself against each. What we want people to feel when they encounter the brand.

**Part 5 — The practical.** Who the brand book is for, what artifacts need to exist by August, what format and source-of-truth the brand system lives in, and how anyone on the team finds and uses the assets.

Each part will be developed with the same care as Part 1 — through founder interviews, in your own language, locked piece by piece rather than imposed top-down.
