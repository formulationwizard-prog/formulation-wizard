# Brand Book Part 2: Voice and Tone

Working draft — May 2, 2026

---

This document captures how Formulation Wizard sounds. Not what the brand says — how it says it. The reference voices it learns from. The vocabulary that's home territory and the vocabulary that's off-limits. The tone, the relationship to the reader, and the writing discipline that holds it all together.

It builds on Part 1 (the foundation) and feeds into everything downstream — marketing copy, product writing, sales materials, long-form content, customer communications, the about page, the homepage hero. Anything written in the brand's name is judged against this document.

The voice was developed through founder interviews, vocabulary sorting, and writing exercises. It is rooted in the founders' own language and tested against the personas (Ram and Paul) the brand serves.

---

## 1. The Central Image

Before the references, before the vocabulary, before the tone — one image holds the voice together.

> **The platform is a clipboard where operators input data to make the products they're making shine.** (verbatim)

A clipboard is a working tool. It's what someone holds while they're doing the work. It doesn't perform; it gets used. It has lines and boxes and check marks. It's plain. It's trusted because it's plain. The product isn't a "platform" or a "solution" — it's the surface the operator writes on while building what they're building.

The brand voice should sound like writing that belongs on or near a clipboard. Not on a brochure. Not on a billboard. On a working surface, used by working people, in service of work that matters.

This image is referenced throughout the rest of this document and should be kept in mind by anyone writing in the brand's voice.

## 2. Reference Voices

The brand learns from five voices and rejects four. The references aren't models to copy — they're calibration points. Writing for Formulation Wizard should feel kindred to the YES list and incompatible with the NO list.

### YES — voices the brand sounds like

**Martha Stewart.** Technically precise. Unhurried. Treats craft as serious work. Assumes the reader is an adult capable of following a real instruction. Domestic authority earned through doing the work, expressed plainly.

**Steve Jobs.** Declarative. Deceptively simple. Contemptuous of bloat. Talks about what something is rather than what it makes you feel. Confident enough to leave space for the reader.

**Mark Cuban.** Direct. Blunt. Peer-to-peer. Zero patience for fluff. Operator-credible because the language is the language of someone who's run things.

**Ezra Klein.** Analytic without being dry. Explains complicated things without flattening them. Generous with the reader, treats them as smart adults capable of following a real argument. The voice for long-form pieces and thoughtful essays where depth serves the reader.

**Rick Bayless.** Technically rigorous and emotionally rooted at the same time. Explains why a particular technique matters, with both the precision of a scholar and the warmth of someone who genuinely cares about getting it right. Demonstrates that technical writing about food can be substantive without being precious.

The unifying quality across all five: **authority earned through doing the work, expressed plainly, with no performance of expertise.** Quiet voices, comparatively — confident without volume.

### NO — voices the brand never sounds like

**Tony Robbins.** Performance of motivation. Voice that earns attention through volume and emotional pressure rather than substance.

**Mr Beast.** Performance of spectacle. Voice built for clicks and reaction, not for trust over time.

**YouTuber bros.** Performance of casualness. The fake friendliness of someone who is actually selling you something while pretending to be your buddy.

**Influencers.** Performance of intimacy. The pretend personal relationship that exists to sell access.

The unifying failure across all four: **voices that perform rather than build.** Voices that earn attention through stagecraft rather than through having done the work.

### Voice register by surface

The five YES references serve different surfaces:

| Surface | Reference | Why |
|---------|-----------|-----|
| Homepage hero, headlines, short product copy | Jobs | Brevity and declarative confidence |
| Product descriptions, technique explanations | Stewart, Bayless | Precise, unhurried instruction |
| Sales materials, peer-to-peer communications | Cuban | Direct, no fluff, operator-credible |
| Long-form essays, thoughtful posts | Klein | Analytic depth, exploratory thinking |
| Founder voice, origin story, craft texture | Bayless | Technical rigor with rooted warmth |

The voice doesn't change identity between surfaces. It changes tempo and depth. Someone reading across all of these should feel the same brand throughout.

## 3. Vocabulary

The brand has a vocabulary spine. Words sort into three buckets: words that are home territory and used freely, words that are off-limits because they break voice, and words that are usable in narrow contexts but never as defaults.

**The criterion (in the founder's own words):** precise, not cliché, not flowery, clear and concise; lands with blue-collar operators, science-technical authors, and brand visionaries.

### GOOD — home territory

**Operator and role vocabulary:** operator, formulator

**Working-tool nouns:** recipe, spec, batch, workflow, platform, dashboard, packet, tool

**Operator verbs:** ship, build, run, integrate

**Ingredients and outputs:** ingredient, label

**Technical and category-specific vocabulary:** formulation, scale-up, hazard analysis, control point, SKU, spec sheet, nutritional panel, ingredient statement, allergen, shelf life, COA (certificate of analysis), PA review, compliance packet, iteration, benchmark, R&D, bench-top, production run, yield optimization, sourcing logic, supplier qualification, lot, batch record, label compliance, cost model, ingredient swap, regulatory map, submission, document trail, version-locked

**Food and process vocabulary:** sourcing, yield, clean, shelf-stable, process, flavor, supply chain, method, lab, functional, trusted, compliance, test

The technical and category-specific vocabulary is not jargon to be avoided — it is the brand's native language. Operators recognize their own language. Visitors who don't know the language will learn it from context. The brand never softens its vocabulary for fear of losing readers; the reader is treated as an adult who works in this world or wants to.

### BAD — off-limits

**SaaS marketing register:** solution, leverage, seamless, unlock, empower, revolutionize, transform, journey, mission-driven, game-changer, world-class, streamline (mostly — see neutral)

**Consumer-food marketing register:** artisan, blend (in marketing register; fine as a technical noun), pantry, small-batch, provenance, heritage, wholesome, handcrafted, premium, premier, hand-selected, pure

**Other:** craft, authentic, kitchen, cook (in marketing register only — fine as a technical verb in instructional contexts)

These twenty-six words are not banned because they're inherently bad. They're banned because they have been emptied by overuse and now signal *this is marketing copy* before the reader has parsed a single sentence. Removing them protects the voice.

Two failure modes are captured: the performative SaaS register (solution, seamless, unlock) and the decorative consumer-food register (artisan, heritage, handcrafted). The brand sounds like neither.

### NEUTRAL — usable in narrow contexts

**streamline** — has a legitimate technical meaning in process improvement contexts; never used as marketing filler.

**tradition** — usable when referring to a specific culinary tradition or long-standing manufacturing practice; misused fast in general copy.

## 4. Tone

The brand's tone is captured in five words. These words are calibration tools. When a writer asks *is this draft on-tone?*, the draft should be readable as all five.

**Direct.** Says what it means. No preamble, no throat-clearing, no buildup. Lands the point in the first sentence when possible.

**Plain.** Unornamented. Doesn't reach for impressive vocabulary. Uses ordinary words in their ordinary senses, except where technical precision requires otherwise.

**Precise.** Technical vocabulary used in its proper sense, never softened, never explained away. Specific nouns over general ones. Specific verbs over abstract ones.

**Unhurried.** Doesn't push. Doesn't perform urgency. Confident enough to take the space it needs to be clear, brief enough to never waste the reader's time on filler.

**Attentive.** Pays attention to the reader. Knows who Ram and Paul are. Notices the specific person on the other end. The brevity works because the brand knew enough about the reader to not need to say more.

### What is deliberately not on this list

Warm. Friendly. Approachable. Professional. Innovative. Passionate. Authentic. Confident.

These were considered and rejected. Warm, friendly, passionate, and authentic are qualities that get *performed* in writing — the moment a brand declares itself warm, it starts producing copy that performs warmth, which breaks the voice we built. Professional, innovative, and confident are claims every brand makes; they're useless as judging tools because every draft can claim to be them.

Warmth, friendliness, and confidence are still present in the brand's voice. They are embedded in brevity, directness, and the trust the brand extends to the reader — not performed through warm-coded language.

## 5. The Relationship

The brand speaks to the reader as a peer.

Specifically: **same side of the work, not same job.** The brand isn't a co-packer. It isn't a product development director. It's the tool. But it's built by people who've been Ram and Paul, who've sat in their meetings, who've carried their problems home. The peer relationship is built on shared understanding of the work, not on doing the same work right now.

### What this means in practice

**No condescension.** The brand doesn't explain things Ram and Paul already know. Vocabulary stays technical. Instructions stay terse. The brand trusts the reader to keep up.

**No deference.** The brand doesn't service-defer (*whatever you need, we're here for you*). Peers don't talk that way. The brand says *here's what the model does, here's where it'll save you time, let us know if you want to walk through it.*

**Willingness to disagree.** A real peer relationship includes the brand pushing back when relevant — *that approach won't work, here's a better one* — without softening it into customer-success language. The brand has a point of view (the four beliefs from Part 1) and peer voice means stating it directly when it serves the reader.

**Assumed competence.** Ram is a director. Paul is operating inside a major manufacturer. The brand writes to them as the experts they are, not as people who need things explained to them.

The two gravitational pulls peer voice has to resist: **teacher voice** (*let me explain how this works*) and **service voice** (*we're here to help with whatever you need*). Both are tempting. Neither is consistent with the brand we built.

## 6. The Writing Discipline

The voice isn't a style to apply. It's a discipline of removal.

Every draft should be edited toward less, not more. The question is not *what should I add to make this feel warm and on-brand*, but *what can I remove until only the necessary thing is left*.

This is the operating principle behind everything else in this document. The five tone words, the vocabulary lists, the relationship — all of them are easier to honor by writing less and harder to honor by writing more. The discipline of removal is what produces voice that actually trusts the reader.

### Specific moves to remove from drafts

- **Paraphrasing what the reader just said.** If a customer told you something, don't repeat it back to them in different words. They know what they said.

- **Narrating the reader's feelings back to them.** *You must feel relieved. I can imagine how stressful that was.* Don't do this. Trust them to feel their own feelings.

- **Statements of company intention toward the reader.** *We wanted you to enjoy this benefit. This is exactly what we built it for.* These performances of care break the voice.

- **Generic acknowledgment phrases.** *Thanks for sharing that. We appreciate your feedback.* Replace with specific acknowledgment when warranted, or remove entirely.

- **Throat-clearing openings.** *I just wanted to say. Just a quick note. Wanted to circle back.* Cut. Start with the actual point.

- **Performative warmth.** *We're so excited to. We're thrilled that.* The voice has warmth without claiming it.

- **Marketing transitions and connectors.** *That said, with that in mind, looking ahead, going forward.* Most of these are filler. Cut and let the sentences sit next to each other.

### Worked example

A customer texts: *"Hey, just wanted to say — finished a production run today and I actually got home at six. Couldn't have done that six months ago. Thanks for building this."*

**Wrong response** (drift toward customer-success register): *"This is the exact benefit we intended users of Formulation Wizard to enjoy. It sounds like you now have peace of mind and can reclaim your time outside of the office while having your tasks stay organized and simplified. I hope you can return to work in the morning stress free and ready to jump in recharged."*

**Right response** (in voice): *"That's great Ram. Thanks for reaching out."*

The right response works because it trusts the customer. It doesn't paraphrase. It doesn't narrate his feelings. It doesn't state company intention. It says *I heard you, I'm glad, thanks for telling us* — and then it stops. The trust is the warmth.

## 7. Phrases to Preserve (Voice Section)

Phrases recorded verbatim during the voice work, to be preserved exactly as written:

> The platform is a clipboard where operators input data to make the products they are making shine.

> Precise, not cliché, not flowery, clear and concise; lands with blue-collar operators, science-technical authors, and brand visionaries.

> That's great. Thanks for reaching out.

> The model you are using has real-time AI-driven pricing, so you should be able to move from modeling to demo quickly with accuracy. Let us know if you'd like to schedule a meeting to walk through how this feature works.

The first phrase (clipboard) is the central image of the brand voice and may be referenced in marketing copy or kept as internal shorthand. The second phrase is the founder's stated criterion and is the operating standard for vocabulary decisions. The third and fourth are the voice in action — small, real, peer-to-peer, no decoration — and serve as templates for how customer-facing communications should sound.
