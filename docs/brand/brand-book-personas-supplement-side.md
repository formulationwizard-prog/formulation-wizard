# Brand Book Part 1 — Supplement Side Personas

Working draft — May 13, 2026

This document extends Brand Book Part 1 (The Foundation) with personas for the supplement industry side of Formulation Wizard's customer base. It is structured to match the Ram and Paul treatment in Part 1, with the same depth of role description, day-to-day work, pain analysis, voice, and value-claim.

The two personas — Dani and Mark — anchor the brand-vs-manufacturer commercial divide that defines most supplement industry relationships. Dani is the brand operator working with contract manufacturers; Mark is the contract manufacturer regulatory lead who serves brand clients. Together they capture both sides of the relationship as potential Formulation Wizard customers.

**Honesty note:** Quotes attributed to Dani and Mark are composite illustrations of supplement industry operator voice patterns. Both personas are built from industry pattern recognition rather than verified against real customer conversations. Validation through real supplement industry conversations is a tracked workstream; verbatim quotes from real customers will be added to these personas as those conversations occur, and persona details may revise based on what validation surfaces.

## Persona 3: Dani

Dani is the Director of Product Development and Regulatory Affairs at a mid-stage supplement brand. The company is 20-80 employees, $5M-$30M in revenue, with 15-30 SKUs across multiple supplement categories — vitamins and minerals, herbals and botanicals, functional formulations. She works with two-to-four contract manufacturers and reports to the founder or COO. She has authority to evaluate and adopt tools. She IS the regulatory affairs department, with maybe one direct report or none.

Dani works across the full arc of the brand's regulated work — from formulation review through commercial launch and on through the compliance maintenance that follows every SKU into the market.

Her formulation work runs alongside the contract manufacturers. She reviews proposed formulations against the DSHEA scope — supplement, not food, not drug. She validates ingredient choices against NDI requirements, calculates Tolerable Upper Intake Level safety margins against proposed serving sizes, checks ingredient compatibility, and manages overage so label claims hold through shelf life.

Her documentation work runs in parallel. She drafts Supplement Facts panels per 21 CFR 101.36. She validates structure/function claims against DSHEA, with the disease-claim line held carefully on every word of every label. She files FDA 30-day notifications when claims warrant. She reviews packaging copy before it prints.

Her manufacturing oversight runs across the two-to-four contract manufacturers her brand uses. She coordinates formulation specifications, reviews COAs on incoming raw materials and finished product, manages third-party testing for identity and potency and contaminants, and maintains supplier qualification documentation per 21 CFR 111.

Her channel work runs across every retailer her brand sells through — each with its own compliance demands, certifications, and audit requirements. She maintains DSHEA documentation files, responds to FDA warning letter trends, tracks adverse event reports, and keeps the brand defensible against the next agency or retailer or buyer who asks her to prove it.

Dani's pain is documentation sprawl. The Supplement Facts panel, the structure/function claim substantiation, the NDI compliance work, the stability and overage calculations, the retail-channel-specific compliance dossiers — all of it lives across email threads, contract manufacturer files, lab portals, and her own internal records. When an auditor or a retail buyer asks her to demonstrate that her work is defensible, she spends hours assembling what should be available in minutes.

The emotional weight is heavier than the operational one. Dani is good at her work — competent, director-level, the orchestrator who keeps her brand defensible against the regulatory complexity that comes with being in the supplement category. But her ability to do the job well depends on documentation she can't fully control. Every audit, every buyer due diligence request, every FDA warning letter trend that reshapes the industry, lands first on her.

What Dani needs is not a replacement for her contract manufacturers, her consultants, or her testing partners. She needs the unified record that makes coordinating with them defensible.

In Dani's own voice (composite illustration): *"I'm not trying to replace my regulatory consultant. She catches things I miss on the novel ingredients and the gray-area claims, and I want her in the loop. But for the routine pre-screening — the obvious disease-claim language, the TUL math on a multi — I shouldn't need to bill her hours. I should be able to handle the routine work myself and bring her the cases that actually need her judgment."*

What the tool gives Dani: routing capacity and defensibility consolidation. She handles the routine regulatory work in-house — Supplement Facts panel assembly, structure/function claim pre-screening, TUL safety math, NDI compliance for established ingredients, stability and overage modeling — with the documentation unified, version-locked, and findable. She brings her regulatory consultant, her contract manufacturer regulatory teams, her testing labs, and her legal counsel into the work where their judgment actually changes the outcome — novel ingredients, gray-area claims, strategic regulatory decisions, complex enforcement responses.

She gets back two things at once. Her professional judgment time, currently consumed by routine pre-screening that the workspace now handles. And her defensive assembly time, currently consumed by reconstructing documentation that the workspace now keeps unified. What she does with that recovered time is the part of her job she got into this work for — the strategic regulatory positioning, the brand defensibility architecture, the work that actually makes her brand harder to challenge.

## Persona 4: Mark

Mark is the Director of R&D and Regulatory Affairs at a mid-tier supplement contract manufacturer. The company is 50-150 employees, $15M-$60M in revenue, serving 30-80 active brand clients across multiple product categories. They produce in multiple dosage forms — capsules, tablets, gummies, powders, sometimes liquids. Mark has a regulatory affairs team of two to four reporting to him, reports to VP of Operations or COO, and has 15-25 years of supplement industry experience. He has authority over formulation and regulatory strategy across the client portfolio.

Mark runs the R&D and regulatory function at a mid-tier supplement contract manufacturer. The company serves 30-80 active brand clients across multiple dosage forms — capsules, tablets, gummies, powders, sometimes liquids — and runs production for each client against client-specific formulation specifications, label requirements, and regulatory positions.

His client engagement work runs at the front end of every new brand relationship. He evaluates the client's proposed formulation against feasibility — what their target potency requires in raw material specifications, what their target shelf life requires in stability and overage modeling, what their target label claims require in DSHEA defensibility. He generates client-facing technical proposals, scopes the regulatory work the engagement will require, and sets expectations on what his team can deliver and what the client owns themselves.

His formulation development work runs alongside his R&D team. They translate the client's spec into a manufacturable formulation, run bench-top samples, validate ingredient compatibility at production scale, calculate overage for shelf life, and prepare the formulation file that production runs against. For complex clients, this work iterates over weeks before the first commercial batch.

His cGMP compliance work runs across every batch his facility produces. Identity testing on incoming raw materials, in-process verification at critical control points, finished product testing, batch record completion per 21 CFR 111, supplier qualification documentation across his ingredient supply chain. His team maintains the documentation that lets the facility pass FDA cGMP inspections and lets his client brands pass their own FDA inspections.

His client-facing regulatory work runs continuously. Brand clients ask him to defend their structure/function claims, to validate their NDI positions, to respond to FDA warning letter trends, to handle adverse event reports that come back from their channels. Some of his clients have sophisticated regulatory teams of their own; others rely on his team to be their regulatory function entirely. He calibrates his support accordingly.

His operational work runs across his team and his facility. He scopes the regulatory affairs team's capacity against incoming client demand, manages the third-party testing labs his facility uses, navigates the audits his facility undergoes from clients and from the FDA, and serves as the regulatory voice in his company's strategic decisions about which client engagements to take and which to decline.

Mark's pain is client context-switching. His team supports 30-80 brand clients, each with their own formulation specifications, their own label requirements, their own testing protocols, their own documentation patterns, their own claim defensibility positions. When his team finishes one client's work and starts another's, they rebuild context every time — which raw materials are qualified for which client, which testing protocols apply, which label compliance requirements govern this batch, which structure/function claims his team has defended for this client and how.

The compounding cost is operational. His regulatory team spends a substantial fraction of their working hours re-orienting to whichever client's work is in front of them. Each engagement consumes more capacity than the work itself requires, because the context-switching overhead doesn't scale linearly with client count.

The strategic cost is heavier. When the sales team brings in a new client, Mark evaluates whether his regulatory function has capacity to add another context to the rotation. Adding clients adds revenue but it adds disproportionate regulatory overhead. The company's growth ceiling sits not in production or sales but in his function's ability to maintain credible regulatory coverage across an expanding client portfolio.

What Mark needs is the operational infrastructure to handle client-specific regulatory context without rebuilding it every time. Not a replacement for the third-party testing labs he uses, the FDA-experienced attorneys his clients consult for novel cases, or the formulator judgment his R&D team applies. The connective tissue that lets his team move across client engagements without the compounding cost.

In Mark's own voice (composite illustration): *"My team is good. The problem isn't competence — it's that every client comes with their own version of every regulatory question, and we rebuild the answer every time we switch from one client's batch to another's. By the time the year ends, I can count the hours we burned just re-orienting. That's the cost that decides whether we can take on the next client or whether we have to say no."*

What the tool gives Mark: operational throughput and client-portfolio capacity. The workspace holds the client-specific context his team currently rebuilds every time they switch engagements — formulation specifications, qualified raw materials, validated structure/function claims, retailer compliance positions, testing protocols, documentation patterns. His team moves between clients without rebuilding the regulatory context every time, because the context is in the workspace, version-locked and current.

His third-party testing labs, his clients' attorneys, his R&D team's formulator judgment, his clients' regulatory consultants — they all stay in the work where their expertise matters. The workspace doesn't replace them. It captures what they produce so his team doesn't burn hours retrieving and reorienting.

He gets back two things. His team's capacity, currently consumed by context-switching overhead that the workspace now absorbs. And his strategic capacity to grow the business — to evaluate new client engagements against his function's actual remaining throughput rather than against the compounding overhead each new client used to add. Mark's regulatory function stops being the growth ceiling his company runs into. It becomes the operational backbone that lets the company grow at the pace its production and sales pipeline can sustain.

## What the Pair Tells Us Together

Dani and Mark capture the two sides of the most common commercial relationship in the supplement industry. Dani owns the brand and orchestrates regulatory work across her contract relationships; Mark IS one of those contract relationships, serving brand clients like Dani's from inside a multi-client production operation.

They share enough that the brand voice can speak to both without splitting: both are mid-tier operators, both have director-level authority, both work in regulated supplement contexts, both face fragmentation pain that scales with their operational range, both work alongside external regulatory professionals whose expertise stays essential.

They differ enough that the brand book has to handle both honestly: Dani's fragmentation is across the parties she orchestrates; Mark's fragmentation is across the clients his team serves. Dani's growth pain is regulatory exposure; Mark's growth pain is team capacity. Dani's relationship with consultants and partners is augmentative; Mark's relationship with his own team and the labs they use is augmentative in a different shape.

Anything written for the Nutraceuticals spoke that lands with both Dani and Mark is on the right track.

The deeper value claim that runs underneath both personas: the workspace gives operators the connective tissue that lets them coordinate regulatory work with the people they already work with — without making any of those people redundant. The consultants stay essential. The contract manufacturers stay essential. The labs stay essential. The attorneys stay essential. The workspace is the substrate that lets the operator coordinate across all of them defensibly.

This augmentation-not-replacement positioning is a deliberate strategic choice. Replacing the supplement industry's existing professional infrastructure would make enemies of the very people who recommend tools to brand operators and contract manufacturers. The brand voice and the spoke copy must hold this positioning throughout.
